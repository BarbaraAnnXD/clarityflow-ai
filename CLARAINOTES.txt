Responsible AI answers for our app
Who could be hurt and how?

Users could be hurt if the system recommends an unrealistic path based on incomplete information. A person with limited money, caregiving responsibilities, or urgent income needs could be pushed toward a path that increases stress or debt.

Who is missing from the data?

Our starting data may miss rural users, low-income users, parents/caregivers, disabled users, people without transportation, people without strong internet access, older career changers, undocumented students, veterans, and people without family support.

Do we need this data at all?

We do not need exact income, exact address, full name, number of children, employer name, documents, or private records. We only need safe ranges like budget pressure, time availability, flexibility needs, risk tolerance, and general location type.

When should a human decide instead?

A human should review before major life decisions like quitting a job, taking on debt, moving cities, enrolling in a costly program, or choosing a path that affects family stability.

Can we explain why the model decides this?

Yes. The app should show the score factors: cost, time, risk, flexibility, credential strength, income speed, and user priorities. The user should see why a path ranked higher.

What can go wrong?

The app could use biased assumptions, outdated pathway data, incomplete user input, overconfident AI wording, or generic career advice that ignores real-life constraints.

Is the output reliable?

It is reliable as a decision-support tool, not as a final answer. It can organize tradeoffs, but it cannot guarantee income, job placement, program quality, or future success.

Can the user verify the answer?

Yes. The app should encourage users to verify costs, job outcomes, program reviews, financial aid, local job demand, and mentor/counselor feedback before acting.

Are limitations clearly stated?

They need to be. The app should say:

“ClarityFlow AI helps compare options and surface tradeoffs. It does not guarantee career outcomes or replace advice from a counselor, mentor, financial advisor, or trusted human.”

## Current ECON Engine

The ECON engine is in:

`econ_mode.py`

It calculates several decision metrics.

### 1. Overall Match / Fit Percent

This shows how well the top career path matches the user’s priorities.

The app weighs each path using:

* income urgency
* budget sensitivity
* flexibility need
* risk aversion
* credential importance

The result is shown as:

`fit_percent`

User-facing label:

**Overall Match**

---

### 2. Recommendation Confidence

This checks how far ahead the top path is compared to the backup path.

If the top path and backup path are close, confidence is lower. This helps prevent the app from sounding too certain when two paths are nearly tied.

Code field:

`confidence_level`

User-facing label:

**Recommendation Confidence**

---

### 3. Life Pressure Level

This estimates how much pressure the user may be under based on:

* income urgency
* budget sensitivity
* flexibility need
* risk aversion

The idea is that users under high pressure should avoid big irreversible decisions and focus on smaller test steps first.

Code fields:

* `pressure_score`
* `pressure_level`

User-facing label:

**Life Pressure Level**

---

### 4. Cost Stress

This estimates how financially stressful a path may be based on the path’s cost and the user’s budget pressure.

A high cost path plus a tight budget creates higher cost stress.

Code field:

`affordability_stress`

User-facing label:

**Cost Stress**

---

### 5. Risk Exposure

This estimates how risky a path may be for the user based on the path’s risk level and the user’s income/budget pressure.

A risky path becomes more dangerous if the user also needs income quickly or has a tight budget.

Code field:

`risk_exposure`

User-facing label:

**Risk Exposure**

---

### 6. Gini-Inspired Decision Clarity Index

This is where we added a real ECON-inspired idea.

The Gini ratio is normally used to measure inequality in a distribution. We adapted that idea to measure how spread out the career path scores are.

In ClarityFlow:

* Low spread means the paths are close together.
* High spread means one path clearly stands out.

This helps the app answer:

**Is there a clear winner, or are the options too close to call?**

Code fields:

* `decision_clarity_index`
* `decision_clarity_level`

User-facing label:

**Decision Clarity**

This is important because it gives the app a responsible AI safety check. If decision clarity is low, the app warns the user to compare the top path and backup path carefully instead of treating the result as final.

---

### 7. Momentum Multiplier

This is another ECON-inspired idea.

In macroeconomics, a multiplier compares the total change in real GDP to the initial change in spending.

We adapted that concept for career decisions.

Instead of:

`Multiplier = Change in Real GDP / Initial Change in Spending`

ClarityFlow uses the idea:

`Momentum Multiplier = Expected Career Movement / Initial Burden`

The goal is to estimate how much career movement a path may create compared to its cost, time, and risk.

Code fields:

* `momentum_multiplier`
* `momentum_level`

User-facing labels:

* **Momentum Multiplier**
* **Multiplier Score**

This helps identify paths that may create useful momentum without requiring too much upfront cost, time, or risk.

Important note: This is not a guaranteed return. It is only a comparison tool.
